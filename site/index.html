RAI1 ASO Targets for Smith-Magenis Syndrome

Computational evidence map for antisense oligonucleotide target selection — a research planning resource, not a drug design tool

What this portal does

This site aggregates public genomic evidence to help researchers evaluate all computationally tractable ASO strategies for RAI1 upregulation in Smith-Magenis Syndrome: uORF blocking (AUG and near-cognate), RNA structure disruption, miRNA site blocking, splice switching, natural antisense targeting, and G-quadruplex resolution. It answers: "Which mechanisms have evidence, which don't, and where should experiments focus?"

What this portal does NOT do

This is not a drug design tool. All evidence is computational. Experimental validation, off-target analysis, and ASO chemistry optimization are required before any therapeutic use.

Key caveats

  • Ribo-seq data is aggregate: Ribosome profiling signal comes from the GWIPS-viz aggregate track, pooling multiple studies across cell types. Neuron-specific Ribo-seq could show a different translational landscape.
  • Haploinsufficiency ceiling: SMS patients have one deleted RAI1 copy. The remaining allele must be upregulated from ~50% to ~70-80% of normal. uORF derepression rarely achieves full derepression, so the therapeutic window may be narrow.
  • Targets scored similarly are effectively tied: Given input uncertainty, targets within ~1 point of each other should be considered equivalent. The ranking suggests priority, not definitive ordering.
  • All coordinates are GRCh38/hg38.

5′UTR Secondary Structure Hotspots

Stable RNA hairpins in the 5′UTR slow ribosome scanning and reduce translation. ASOs that melt these structures can increase protein output, complementary to uORF blocking. Windows scored by minimum free energy (MFE) using ViennaRNA. More negative = more stable = better ASO target.

5 150-210 -31.1 -0.518 86.7% Open

The most stable region (pos 190-250, MFE = -36.1 kcal/mol) overlaps with the canonical uORF at position 262. This overlap has two implications: (1) the stable structure may slow ribosome scanning, increasing the probability that ribosomes engage the uORF rather than reading through to the main AUG, making this uORF more repressive than its weak Kozak (1/3) would suggest; (2) an ASO targeting this region could simultaneously disrupt the structure AND block the uORF start codon, a dual-mechanism approach. Importantly, structure disruption works regardless of whether translation is cap-dependent or IRES-mediated, making it a safer bet than uORF blocking alone given the IRES risk flagged below.

RAI1 has a relatively short 3′UTR (1,472 nt) with only 6 conserved miRNA families. The strongest (gga-miR-144-3p, context++ = -0.473) has moderate predicted repression. Tissue caveat: miR-144-3p is primarily expressed in hematopoietic cells, not neurons. Its functional relevance in the brain (the target tissue for SMS) is uncertain. Additionally, blocking a miRNA site with a steric blocker requires competing with Argonaute-loaded RISC, a fundamentally different pharmacological challenge than 5′UTR uORF blocking. miRNA site blocking is a weak strategy for RAI1.

G-Quadruplex Scan: Negative

No G-quadruplex motifs (G3+N1-7G3+N1-7G3+N1-7G3+) found in the canonical 5′UTR despite 78% GC content. The GC richness is distributed, not in clustered G-runs. G4-resolving ASOs are not applicable for RAI1.

IRES Risk Assessment: High (5/5 features)

The RAI1 5′UTR scores 5/5 on IRES-associated features: long leader (484 bp), high GC (78%), highly structured (MFE -0.6/nt), polypyrimidine tract at position 210, and 262 nt cap-to-AUG distance. If RAI1 uses cap-independent (IRES-mediated) translation, uORF blocking would be ineffective because ribosomes would bypass uORFs entirely.

However: No RAI1 IRES activity has been reported (zero publications). The same features are also consistent with a difficult-to-scan UTR where uORFs have maximum regulatory impact. The Ribo-seq signal at the canonical uORF (68% of CDS levels) is more consistent with cap-dependent scanning than IRES-mediated internal entry.

To resolve: Include a bicistronic reporter construct in the Phase 1 validation. Place the RAI1 5′UTR between two reporter genes. If downstream reporter is expressed without a functional upstream cap, IRES activity is confirmed and the uORF strategy should be deprioritized in favor of structure-disruption ASOs (which work regardless of translation mechanism).
